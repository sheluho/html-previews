Frequently Asked Questions

Common inquiries about whole home remodeling and hiring a general contractor.

What is a turnkey renovation?

A turnkey renovation is a comprehensive service where a general contractor manages every single aspect of your home remodeling project. From the initial design and permitting phase through construction, plumbing, electrical, and final finishing, the contractor handles it all. You essentially "turn the key" to enter your fully completed, renovated home without having to manage multiple sub-contractors yourself.

Do I need a general contractor for a home addition?

Yes, structural changes like a home addition almost always require a licensed general contractor. Additions involve complex foundation work, structural engineering, roof integration, and tying into existing electrical and plumbing systems. A general contractor ensures the work meets local building codes and passes all necessary municipal inspections.

How long does whole home remodeling usually take?

The timeline for whole home remodeling varies significantly based on the scope of work and square footage. A standard interior overhaul might take 3 to 5 months, while extensive structural changes or major home additions can extend the timeline to 6 to 9 months or more. A professional general contractor will provide a detailed timeline during the planning phase.

What is the first step in planning a renovation?

The critical first step is establishing your goals and budget, followed immediately by consulting with a design-build firm or a general contractor. Before any demolition begins, you need accurate architectural blueprints, a finalized design plan, and secured municipal permits. Proper planning prevents costly changes mid-construction.

Can I live in my house during a whole home renovation?

While it is sometimes possible to live in the home during minor updates, it is generally not recommended during a true whole home remodeling project. Major renovations involve significant dust, noise, water/power shutoffs, and safety hazards. Relocating temporarily often allows the general contractor to complete the work faster and more safely.
